The Journey

A private circle through fire and ice

Nine nights with your own driver-guide and a private vehicle, tracing the south and west of Iceland at the start of aurora season. Three bases, each with its own character: the geothermal calm of Reykholt in Borgarfjörður, the dramatic coastline at Vík, and the creative energy of Reykjavík. Every touring day is private. Every evening, the sky is dark enough for Northern Lights.

Day 1
Reykjavík → Borgarfjörður
Fosshotel Reykholt
Night 1 of 3  ·  B&B  ·  Superior Room

Your driver-guide meets you at Keflavík Airport and heads north into West Iceland. The route passes through Hvalfjörður — the “Whale Fjord” — with steep mountains rising on both sides. Further on, Hraunfossar appears: a long sequence of springs emerging directly from the lava field and cascading into the Hvítá River, with no single dramatic drop, just the whole hillside quietly releasing water. Barnafoss, its neighbour, surges through a narrow rocky gorge with its own darker folklore. Arrive at Fosshotel Reykholt by late afternoon — a modern country hotel set in the historic village once home to the medieval poet Snorri Sturluson.

Day 2
Reykholt  ·  West Iceland
Fosshotel Reykholt
Night 2 of 3  ·  B&B

A day without a schedule. Fosshotel Reykholt sits in the scenic Borgarfjörður countryside with the Deildartunguhver hot springs a short drive away — the most powerful hot spring in Europe. Hiking trails wind through the surrounding lava fields, or you can take a taxi to Krauma Geothermal Bath (seven minutes) for a proper Icelandic spa afternoon. The hotel’s own wellness area has outdoor hot and cold tubs, a sauna, and a steam bath. October light in this valley has a particular quality. Slow down and watch for the aurora after dinner.

Day 4
þingvellir → South Coast → Vík
Hotel Vík
Night 1 of 3  ·  B&B  ·  Superior Room, Upper Floor

Your driver-guide picks you up for the southward journey. First stop: þingvellir National Park, a UNESCO site where the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates pull apart. Then Urriðafoss — one of Iceland’s widest waterfalls by volume — before the iconic Seljalandsfoss, where a path takes you behind the cascade itself. Gljúfrabúi, the hidden waterfall tucked inside a narrow canyon, is just a few minutes further. Most visitors walk straight past it. Arrive at Hotel Vík by evening, with views toward the Reynisdrangar sea stacks and the black sand beach.

Day 5
Vík  ·  Katla  ·  Black Beach
Hotel Vík
Night 2 of 3  ·  B&B

Morning brings the Katla Ice Cave Tour — a three-hour expedition beneath Iceland’s most powerful subglacial volcano. A Super Jeep takes you up onto the glacier, then crampons and a helmet take you into chambers where the walls shift from black volcanic ash to luminous blue ice. The afternoon is yours at Reynisfjara Beach: the Atlantic crashing against basalt columns, the Reynisdrangar sea stacks rising from black sand, and Dyrhólaey peninsula visible to the west. Optionally, a one-hour horseback ride along the black beach with Vík Horse Adventures — gentle, suited for beginners, on sure-footed Icelandic horses.

Day 6
Glacier Lagoon  ·  Diamond Beach  ·  Canyons
Hotel Vík
Night 3 of 3  ·  B&B

The longest touring day — and one that stays with you. Drive east through Eldhraun, the vast moss-covered lava field from the 1783 Laki eruption, then past Fjaðrárgljúfur canyon with its 100-metre moss-clad cliffs. Jökulsárlón Glacier Lagoon is the destination: icebergs calving from Breiðamerkurjökull and drifting slowly toward the sea. Diamond Beach follows, where those same icebergs wash ashore on black volcanic sand and catch the low October light. Optionally, a boat tour on the quieter Fjallsárlón lagoon (75–90 min) brings you among the floating ice with Öræfajökull — Iceland’s tallest volcano — towering above. On the return, stop at þakgil — a hidden valley deep in South Iceland’s dramatic landscapes — and Gígjagjá, the Yoda Cave, with its distinctive Star Wars silhouette.

Day 7
Skógar → Reykjavík
Sand Hotel by Keahotels
Night 1 of 3  ·  B&B  ·  Superior Room

Your driver-guide brings you back west along the south coast with final stops at Skógafoss — 60 metres wide, rainbows in the mist on sunny days — and an optional visit to the Raufarhólshellir Lava Tunnel, one of Iceland’s longest and most accessible lava tubes. The one-hour standard tour walks you through chambers lit to reveal the volcanic colours of the tunnel walls. By afternoon you’re in Reykjavík, checking into the Sand Hotel on Laugavegur — the main shopping street, perfectly placed for the harbour, the restaurants, and the city’s creative pulse.

Day 8
Reykjavík
Sand Hotel by Keahotels
Night 2 of 3  ·  B&B

A full day to explore Reykjavík at your own pace. Start with Hallgrímskirkja — the 73-metre church that defines the city’s skyline, its architecture inspired by the basalt columns found all across Iceland. Walk down to Harpa Concert Hall on the harbour, its geometric glass facade constantly shifting colour. The Sun Voyager sculpture catches the light at the waterfront. Höfði House, where Reagan and Gorbachev met in 1986. The Alþingishúsð parliament building and the colourful streets of Reykjavík Old Town. The city has a different quality in October — fewer visitors, sharper light, warmer cafés.
