Why East Africa

A whole new chapter

Having experienced southern Africa, you already know how special a safari can be. East Africa offers something beautifully different. The scale here is unlike anywhere else, and the two experiences at the heart of this journey simply cannot be found in quite the same way anywhere else on the planet.

Picture two million animals moving across open plains, following ancient instincts and the promise of rain. Then picture yourself sitting quietly in ancient forest, just a few feet from a family of mountain gorillas going about their day. These are not experiences you read about and then move on from. They stay with you.

This is a journey designed to feel like a true discovery, not simply a repeat of what you have seen before.

The Great Migration

The greatest show
on earth

Every year, around 1.2 million wildebeest and 300,000 zebra move through Tanzania and Kenya in a continuous 800-kilometre circuit, following the rains and the promise of fresh grass. They are joined by hundreds of thousands of topi, gazelle and impala, bringing the total to over two million animals moving together in an ancient, unbroken cycle.

What makes a migration safari so different from a traditional game drive is the sheer sense of life and movement around you. Predators follow the herds. Calves are born on the open plains. The Mara River runs with crocodiles. Every morning brings something new, and no two days feel the same.

"With up to 1,000 animals per square kilometre, the great columns of wildebeest can even be seen from space."

The Mara River Crossings

The river crossings are the moment most people have in mind when they dream of the migration. Thousands of wildebeest gathering on the bank, hesitating, then committing in a sudden, thunderous surge into the crocodile-filled water below. It is raw, chaotic, and completely captivating. July through October is when these crossings are most frequent, which makes a Q3 trip very well timed.

A gentle note on the experience: Nature sets the schedule here, not us. The wildebeest decide when and where to cross, and it cannot be predicted. You may spend a morning at the river with nothing happening, then return in the afternoon to something you will never forget. This is part of what makes it so special. We always recommend building in enough time at each camp to let the experience unfold naturally.
December – March

Calving Season

400,000 calves are born in just a few weeks on the southern Serengeti plains. Predators converge from miles around. Extraordinary for big cat sightings.

May – June

The Trek North

Columns of wildebeest stretch for kilometres as they move north. The first river challenge: the Grumeti. Fewer tourists, lower rates.

July – October

River Crossing Season

The peak spectacle. Herds gather at the Mara River in the northern Serengeti and push north into Kenya. Daily crossings possible at the Mara and Talek rivers.

November

The Return South

Short rains begin and herds cross back south through the eastern Serengeti — another opportunity to witness the crossings in reverse.

Where to Go

Tanzania or Kenya?

It is a fair question, and one we are always happy to walk through. Both destinations are genuinely special, and the right choice depends on how you want to travel and what you want to see.

Kenya's Masai Mara is one of the most iconic names in safari travel, and deservedly so. The reserve has a wonderful energy, outstanding wildlife year-round, and a rich cultural dimension through the Maasai communities who call the region home. If Kenya is on your list, it is absolutely worth your time.

That said, the Mara River, which features in just about every migration documentary you have ever watched, actually runs mostly through Tanzania. The bulk of the wildebeest herd spends most of the year in the Serengeti, which covers a much larger area and offers a broader, more expansive experience of the migration as a whole.

There is also a practical reason to anchor the safari portion in Tanzania. Combining both countries in one trip adds complexity, as you would typically need to fly back through Nairobi to connect between them. Tanzania flows naturally into Uganda for the gorilla trekking leg, with straightforward cross-border light aircraft connections that keep the journey seamless.

Best window Mid-July – October

One thing worth knowing about the Serengeti

  • The Serengeti is a national park, which means it is open to all operators. During peak migration season, you may find yourself sharing a sighting with other vehicles nearby. This is simply the nature of a national park, and quite different from the private reserves and conservancies you may have experienced in southern Africa. It is worth keeping in mind, though it takes nothing away from the wildlife itself.
  • For a more exclusive feel, conservancy options adjacent to the Masai Mara in Kenya are worth considering. We are happy to talk through what makes most sense for you.

Gorilla Trekking

A once-in-a-lifetime encounter

Coming from the open drama of the Serengeti, gorilla trekking offers a beautifully different kind of experience. You move through dense, ancient forest, following your guide to wherever the gorilla family has settled that morning. The hike can take anywhere from one hour to several, depending on the day. And then, quite suddenly, you find them.

The hour you spend with the gorillas tends to pass very quickly. They are utterly unhurried, going about their day with complete indifference to your presence. Juveniles tumble and play. Adults groom each other quietly. The silverback keeps watch. Many of our clients tell us it is the most moving wildlife experience of their lives, and we would agree. There is something about that hour in the forest that is genuinely hard to put into words.

Alternative

Rwanda
Volcanoes National Park

Very well-organised, polished logistics, excellent infrastructure. Kigali is a genuinely impressive city worth a night. Premium positioning has made it the most famous gorilla destination globally.

Permit: USD 1,500 per person

Our Recommendation

Uganda
Bwindi Impenetrable Forest

Home to roughly half of the world's remaining mountain gorillas. Intimate luxury lodges set in one of Africa's most ancient forests. The trek is wilder and the setting is more dramatic.

Permit: USD 800 per person

Why we suggest Uganda, and why we recommend going on 2 consecutive days

  • The gorilla trekking experience in Uganda is comparable to Rwanda in every meaningful way: same species, same format, the same extraordinary hour with a habituated family. The main difference is the permit cost. At USD 800 per person in Uganda versus USD 1,500 in Rwanda, you are not paying for a better experience in Rwanda, just a different address.
  • We recommend booking two permits on consecutive days, which means staying three nights at the lodge. The first trek tends to be overwhelming in the best possible way. The second day is when you truly settle in, slow down, and take it all in. The difference between day one and day two is something most people do not expect but are very glad for.
  • Bwindi has some of the finest small lodges in East Africa. The setting is intimate and deeply atmospheric, all mist and ancient forest and volcanic ridgelines, which makes for a lovely contrast after the wide open drama of the Serengeti plains.

Suggested Shape

A 13-day journey

This is a suggested shape for the journey, designed to give you a feel for how it might flow. Every element can be adjusted to suit your pace, your interests and your budget. We have put Uganda first and Tanzania second quite deliberately: the cross-border connections work smoothly in this direction, and there is something lovely about moving from the quiet intimacy of the forest into the vast, open drama of the Serengeti.

1Arrival

Arrive Entebbe, Uganda

Settle in on arrival with an overnight in Entebbe. If time allows the following morning, a visit to Ngamba Island Chimpanzee Sanctuary on Lake Victoria makes for a gentle and memorable start to the trip.

2–4Uganda

Bwindi Impenetrable Forest — 3 nights

Fly in to the nearest airstrip and transfer to your lodge, set deep in the forest. Days 3 and 4 are your gorilla trek days. Two consecutive permits give you the time to experience it properly: once to take it all in, and once to truly savour it.

5–7Tanzania

Northern Serengeti — 3 nights

A short cross-border flight brings you into Tanzania and on to the northern Serengeti. Two full days of game drives, with mornings and evenings spent near the Mara River during peak crossing season. We recommend a private vehicle so you can set your own pace and stay at the river as long as you like.

8–10Tanzania

Ngorongoro Conservation Area — 3 nights

Fly south to the Ngorongoro Highlands and descend into the crater, one of the most wildlife-rich places on the continent. Black rhino, elephant, lion and vast herds of plains game share this ancient caldera. For those who enjoy a good walk, the Empakaai Crater hike is a wonderful way to spend an afternoon.

11–13Optional

Zanzibar — 2 nights extension

A short flight via Arusha brings you to Zanzibar for a gentle wind-down. Spend a half day wandering the spice-scented alleys of Stone Town, then settle into two quiet nights by the water. A lovely way to ease back into the world after 10 days of extraordinary wildlife.

A few practical notes

  • Getting thereEthiopian Airlines is our preferred routing from Singapore, flying via Addis Ababa to Entebbe or Kilimanjaro. It is a Star Alliance partner, well-connected, and avoids the longer Middle East hub options.
  • BudgetPrices start from around SGD 1,000 per person per night for comfortable safari accommodation, excluding park fees and permits. In some locations, a budget of USD 1,000 per person per night gives you noticeably more comfort and a wider choice of camps. Q3 is peak season, so this is reflected in the rates.
  • Gorilla permitsPermits are limited and we recommend securing them early. In Uganda, the cost is USD 800 per person per trek.
  • LuggageLight aircraft transfers have a soft bag requirement, typically around 15kg per person. Worth keeping in mind when you pack.
  • Book earlyMigration season is high season across East Africa and the best camps fill up well in advance. We would love to help you get this locked in sooner rather than later.
